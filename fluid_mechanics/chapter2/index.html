    Chapter 2: Fluids in Equilibrium¶

    1. Hydrostatic Pressure¶

    Hydrostatic Pressure

    Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure exerted by a fluid at equilibrium due to the force of gravity. In a static fluid, pressure increases with depth.

    \[P = P_0 + \rho g h\]

    Where:

    • \(P\) = absolute pressure at depth \(h\)
    • \(P_0\) = pressure at the surface (reference)
    • \(\rho\) = fluid density
    • \(g\) = acceleration due to gravity (9.81 m/sÂ²)
    • \(h\) = depth from the surface

    This assumes the fluid is incompressible and at rest. Hydrostatic pressure is important in analyzing forces on submerged surfaces like gates, dams, and tanks.

    Total force on a submerged vertical surface:

    \[F = \rho g \bar{h} A\]

    Where \(\bar{h}\) is the depth to the centroid and \(A\) is the surface area.

    Example: Pressure at Depth¶

    Problem 1

    A container is filled with oil of density \(\rho = 850 \, \text{kg/m}^3\). What is the pressure at a depth of \(h = 3 \, \text{m}\)? Assume atmospheric pressure is not included.

    Solution:

    \[P = \rho g h = 850 \times 9.81 \times 3 = 25,027.5 \, \text{Pa}\]

    Answer: \(P = 25,027.5 \, \text{Pa}\)

    Another example: Find the pressure 5 m below water (\(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)) open to atmospheric pressure \(P_0 = 101325 \, \text{Pa}\):

    \[P = 101325 + (1000)(9.81)(5) = 150,375 \, \text{Pa}\]

    2. Pascalâ€™s Law¶

    Pascal's Law

    Pascal's Law states that the pressure applied to a confined incompressible fluid is transmitted undiminished in all directions.

    \[\frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{F_2}{A_2} = \Delta P\]

    Where:

    • \(F_1, F_2\) = forces on two pistons
    • \(A_1, A_2\) = areas of the pistons
    • \(\Delta P\) = pressure transmitted equally

    Output force formula:

    \[F_2 = F_1 \frac{A_2}{A_1}\]

    source

    Example:

    • Small piston: \(A_1 = 0.01~\text{m}^2\), \(F_1 = 100~\text{N}\)
    • Large piston: \(A_2 = 0.5~\text{m}^2\)
    \[F_2 = 100 \cdot \frac{0.5}{0.01} = 5000~\text{N}\]

    3. Manometers and Pressure Measurement¶

    Manometer Principle

    source

    Measures pressure difference via fluid height difference:

    \[\Delta P = \rho g h\]

    Where:

    • \(\rho\) = density of fluid
    • \(g\) = acceleration due to gravity
    • \(h\) = height difference

    Example:

    Mercury (\(\rho = 13600 \ \text{kg/m}^3\)) in U-tube shows \(h = 0.15 \ \text{m}\):

    \[\Delta P = 13600 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 0.15 \approx 19,998 \ \text{Pa}\]

    4. Hydrostatic Forces on Plane and Curved Surfaces¶

    Hydrostatic Force on a Plane Surface

    Total hydrostatic force on a vertical plane:

    \[F = \rho g \bar{h} A\]
    • \(F\) = total force
    • \(\bar{h} = h_\text{top} + \frac{h}{2}\) = depth of centroid
    • \(A\) = area of surface

    Center of pressure:

    \[h_{cp} = \bar{h} + \frac{I_G}{\bar{h} A}\]

    Moments of inertia \(I_G\):

    • Rectangle (base \(b\), height \(h\)): \(I_x = \frac{b h^3}{12}\)
    • Circle (radius \(R\)): \(I = \frac{\pi R^4}{4}\)
    • Triangle (base \(b\), height \(h\)): \(I_x = \frac{b h^3}{36}\)

    Example: Vertical Gate¶

    A rectangular gate: \(b=2~\text{m}\), \(h=4~\text{m}\), submerged with top edge \(h_\text{top} = 1~\text{m}\).

    1. Depth to centroid:

      \[\bar{h} = 1 + 4/2 = 3~\text{m}\]

    2. Hydrostatic force:

      \[F = 1000 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 3 \cdot (2 \cdot 4) = 235,440~\text{N}\]

    3. Moment of inertia:

      \[I_G = \frac{2 \cdot 4^3}{12} = 10.67~\text{m}^4\]

    4. Center of pressure:

      \[h_{cp} = 3 + \frac{10.67}{3 \cdot 8} = 3.44~\text{m}\]

    Hydrostatic Forces on Curved Surfaces

    For curved submerged surfaces:

    • Horizontal component: \(F_H = \rho g \bar{h} A_v\)
    • Vertical component: \(F_V = \rho g V_f\)
    • Resultant: \(F_R = \sqrt{F_H^2 + F_V^2}\), \(\theta = \tan^{-1}(F_V/F_H)\)

    5. Buoyancy and Archimedes' Principle¶

    Buoyancy

    Buoyant force is the upward force due to pressure difference:

    • Depends on fluid density and displaced volume
    • For floating bodies, only a portion of volume is displaced
    • Applies to gases as well

    Example: Flat Plate¶

    Flat plate: thickness \(s=0.05~\text{m}\), area \(A=2~\text{m}^2\), submerged at \(h=1~\text{m}\):

    \[P_\text{top} = 1000 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 1 = 9810~\text{Pa}$$ $$P_\text{bottom} = 1000 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 1.05 = 10300.5~\text{Pa}\]
    \[F_\text{top} = 9810 \cdot 2 = 19620~\text{N}$$ $$F_\text{bottom} = 10300.5 \cdot 2 = 20601~\text{N}\]
    \[F_b = F_\text{bottom} - F_\text{top} = 981~\text{N}\]

    Displaced fluid: \(V = 0.05 \cdot 2 = 0.1~\text{m}^3\)
    Weight: \(1000 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 0.1 = 981~\text{N}\)

    Archimedes' Principle¶

    \[F_b = \rho_f g V_d\]
    • \(F_b\) = buoyant force
    • \(V_d\) = volume of fluid displaced

    Conditions:

    • Floats: \(F_b \ge W\)
    • Sinks: \(F_b < W\)
    • Neutral: \(F_b = W\)

    Example: Floating Wooden Block¶

    Volume: \(0.1~\text{m}^3\)

    \[F_b = 1000 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 0.1 = 981~\text{N}\]

    Example: Sinking Metal Cube¶

    Volume: \(0.02~\text{m}^3\), density \(7800~\text{kg/m}^3\)

    • Buoyant force: \(196.2~\text{N}\)
    • Weight: \(1529.2~\text{N}\)
    • Sinks since \(W > F_b\)

    6. Stability of Floating and Submerged Bodies¶

    Stability Criteria

    Key points:

    • \(G\) = center of gravity
    • \(B\) = center of buoyancy
    • \(M\) = metacenter

    Conditions:

    • Stable: \(M\) above \(G\)
    • Unstable: \(M\) below \(G\)
    • Neutral: \(M = G\)

    Metacentric height:

    \[GM = BM - BG, \quad BM = \frac{I}{V}\]

    Example: Floating Pontoon¶

    Base: \(5 \times 3~\text{m}\), displaced water \(V = 4~\text{m}^3\), CG = 0.6 m above base

    \[ I = \frac{3 \cdot 5^3}{12} = 31.25~\text{m}^4 $$ $$ BM = \frac{31.25}{4} = 7.8125~\text{m} $$ $$ GM = 7.8125 - 0.6 = 7.21~\text{m (Stable)} \]
